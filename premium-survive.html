Most business owners have no decision-making process for normal times. In a crisis — when time is short, information is incomplete, and consequences are severe — they have even less. The result is paralysis, panic, or the wrong decision made fast. This section gives you a process that works precisely when everything else isn't.
The framework
The 5-step decision process — under pressure.
1
Write your Question Statement
Define the problem before you try to solve it. In a crisis, the urgency to act often means solving the wrong problem entirely. Write it down. The act of writing forces clarity.
In a crisis: "What is the one decision, if made well, that changes everything right now?"
2
Challenge everything
Intuition, assumptions, biases, incomplete information. In normal times these distort decisions. In a crisis they can be catastrophic. What do you actually know? What are you assuming? What are the 188 cognitive biases that might be at work right now?
In a crisis: Strip the assumptions. What is actually true right now, not what you feared would be true?
3
Identify alternatives
There is almost never only one path. Map the alternatives — choices, risks, and consequences of each. The first workable option is rarely the best one, even under time pressure.
In a crisis: What are the three paths? What does each one cost if it goes wrong?
4
Implement
Choose, communicate, and act. The best decision implemented slowly is often worse than a good decision implemented fast. Communicate the decision clearly — to the team, to clients, to suppliers. Uncertainty kills confidence.
In a crisis: Decide now. Communicate immediately. Silence is more damaging than imperfect information.
5
Create a feedback loop
Monitor, review, pivot. The decision you made yesterday may need to change today as new information arrives. A crisis is not a single event — it is a sequence of decisions. Stay in the loop.
In a crisis: Review daily. What has changed? What do you know now that you didn't know yesterday?

The ABCD client ranking — when you need to cut
From the book. In a survival situation you cannot serve everyone equally. Know which clients to protect and which to release.
A
Awesome
High value, great to work with, pays on time, refers others.
Protect at all costs. Personal contact. Priority service.
B
Basic
Routine, stable, good relationships. The solid middle.
Maintain service. Honest communication about your situation.
C
Can't deal with
Drains time, complains, pays late, demands more than they pay for.
Release with grace. The time they free up goes to A clients.
D
Dead
Unprofitable, difficult, or already inactive.
Release immediately. Do not spend another hour on D clients in a crisis.
