Practice Framework
The 60|40 Lawyer
The lens every quarter of this plan is filtered through. A law firm principal who spends all their time fee-earning is building a job, not a business. The 60|40 split is the operating model that creates a practice which grows without requiring more of Patrick personally.
The 60|40 Principle · Practice Leadership
The firm grows at the rate Patrick stops being the bottleneck.
Most law firm principals operate at 90%+ fee-earning and wonder why the business doesn't grow. The 60|40 model says: spend 60% of your time on client work, and 40% on the business — its people, its marketing, its systems, and its future. That 40% is not overhead. It is the work that makes everything else scale.

① Time
Audit & Reclaim
Understanding where Patrick's hours actually go — before deciding what to keep
  • Q1Patrick completes a 2-week time audit: every task logged against fee-earning, operations, business development, or leadership. No judgement — just data. The gap between perceived and actual time split is usually confronting.
  • Q2The audit becomes the baseline. Patrick tracks his leadership time percentage monthly — one number. The goal is visibility before optimisation.
  • Q3Quarterly review of time split trend. Is the leadership percentage growing? If not, the bottleneck is structural — systems aren't ready, not willingness.
  • Q4Full-year time data reviewed. What did Patrick spend the year doing, and is it what a business owner should spend their time on?
The lever: What gets measured changes. A principal who has never tracked their time split cannot make intentional choices about it. The audit is not administrative — it is the first act of ownership.
② Delegation
What Only Patrick Can Do
Identifying and protecting the work that truly requires Patrick — releasing everything else
  • Q1The delegation inventory: every task Patrick performs in a week categorised as "only Patrick can do this" vs "someone else could do this with the right system." Most lists surprise people — the second category is larger than expected.
  • Q2One significant responsibility moved to Deanne and not taken back. The test: Deanne makes a decision without asking Patrick, and the outcome is acceptable. That's the first delegation success.
  • Q3Deanne is making operational decisions independently. Patrick is consulted, not required. The coaching focus moves to what Patrick does with the time recovered.
  • Q4Review the delegation map: what does Patrick own now vs twelve months ago? The gap is the year's leadership work — it should be substantial.
The lever: Delegation without systems is just abdication — things fall through and Patrick gets pulled back in. Delegation with systems is leverage. Every SOP Deanne owns is time Patrick doesn't spend repeating himself.
③ Systems
Infrastructure for Independence
Building the practice infrastructure that makes delegation safe and growth sustainable
  • Q1Core matter SOPs written into LEAP: file opening, VOI checklist, settlement workflow. These documents mean new work doesn't require Patrick's verbal instructions every time. Written once, used always.
  • Q2Monthly financial reporting template live. Patrick reviews the numbers in under 30 minutes each month — not because he doesn't care, but because the format is right and the data is ready.
  • Q3Referral tracking system in place. Every referral source is acknowledged and tracked. The system makes referral cultivation feel like management, not relationship guilt.
  • Q4Systems audit: what is still held in Patrick's head that should be in a document, a checklist, or a LEAP workflow? Year 2 starts with fewer single points of failure.
The lever: A firm whose operations live in the principal's head is not a firm — it is a practice that stops when the principal stops. Systems are what convert Patrick's expertise into institutional knowledge.
④ Vision
Thinking Forward, Not Just Through Today
Patrick's ability to work on the firm's future, not just manage its present
  • Q1Patrick articulates — in writing — what Sheridan Legal looks like in three years. Not a financial projection. A description: how does it operate, who is in the team, what does Patrick's week look like? The picture precedes the plan.
  • Q2Patrick identifies one decision that only an owner makes — about the firm's future, not its files. A new service, a fee increase, a team addition. He makes it and owns it.
  • Q3Marketing and referral strategy reviewed with a 12-month lens. Patrick is thinking about where clients will come from next year, not just this month.
  • Q4Year 2 plan written. Not a wish list — a plan: targets, channels, team structure, and Patrick's role. The question asked explicitly: "What does full look like, and do I want it?"
The lever: Principals who are too busy to think about next year are usually too busy because they didn't think about it last year. Vision work is not a luxury — it is what prevents the firm from being reactive indefinitely.

Coaching priority · Q4

Review, raise, and define Year 2. The coaching focus shifts from behaviour to strategy. By Q4, Patrick has a year of real data — matters, fees, referral sources, team performance, and his own time split. The question is: what does Year 2 look like if he chooses to grow further, and what does it look like if he optimises for margin and lifestyle instead? Both are valid answers. The coaching role is to help Patrick choose from evidence, not habit. Raise fees where the market supports it. Formalise what's working. Decide what full looks like — and whether that's where he wants to go.
